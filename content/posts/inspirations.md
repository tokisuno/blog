# introduction
I don't think I am a very sociable. I try my best to make sure people feel welcome while also trying to look out for myself. I spent a long time trying to be someone I wasn't and along the way it caused me a lot of stress, anguish, and a loss of self. I would find someone that I thought was cool, obsess over them, try and do everything they do, and then move on after 1-2 weeks. Sometimes even hours. If that isn't the most ADHD thing you've heard I don't know what is.

Anyways, I was talking in a discord with some people and someone (who i believe is as stubborn as i am) talked about how they don't have people they look up to because it's somewhat substandard. Their reasons honestly went in line with how I was in the past, when I was weaker and infinitely more insecure than I am now. I think the message behind what they said is true, but taken to an extreme. This blog post will be me basically summing up how I look up to people without turning into a shell of a human being.

I'd also like to preface this with the following: when I talk about people I look up to, I also mean inspirations. People who have inspired me in one way or another are in the same category. Does this mean I think everything they do is correct, and I am a total simp/yes-man to everything they do and/or say? Of course not, and it would be ignorant to assume so. People and interactions are more nuanced than that. It isn't black and white, it isn't binary. We are made up of things we think are cool and interesting because someone else did it. I think that is unavoidable. Even some of my smaller interests were all kick-started by looking up to someone at some point, and I don't think there's a problem with so long as you don't become obsessive and try to justify everything they do because they're your "idol".

# perspective of "eh it's fine"
## how it started
This is honestly my perspective. LOL.

Let me give a brief history as to how I would handle this in the past. When I was younger, I was a hardcore gamer, but I didn't know how to get good. I was also afraid of getting good in fear of my friends not wanting to play with me anymore (as that is a common story amongst many others). So I always strived to be an average middle-ground, to be someone who wouldn't bottom frag while still being somewhat impactful for the team. I had an obsession with settings, and different sensitivities. I would always change my settings to other pro players', change resolutions to copy them, etc. It was cringe, and when I would get comfortable, I'd switch again. What does this have to do with anything? Everything actually.

I spent more time thinking about resolutions, sensitivities, and how to be someone I wasn't without trying to figure out who I was, what I was good at, what I was bad at, and how to take loss. My mentality was weak and fragile. My obsession with the people I looked up to costed me years of improvement in areas I still consider to be my favourite, and it's somewhat tilting to think about. I didn't know how to handle having inspirations, or how to look up to someone. 

## how i am now
I have quite a bit of people I look up to for various reasons. The only difference now is my mentality towards it, and towards myself. This was pre-medicated and pre-therapy Lucas, so my brain was all over the place. For me at least, taking SSRIs helped me a lot. I could finally think about what I wanted to think about, and let me see the bigger picture more clearly. Granted, I still have issues with seeing the bigger picture. This apparently has a lot to do with autism but I digress. 

The point is, I found a middle-ground. I am able to look up to people without it getting out of hand, or losing track of who I am as a person like I did in the past.
